## SECTION 1 — NA MED AESTHETICS PLATFORM UX PATTERNS

### 1.1 Information Hierarchy on Provider Listings (RealSelf Model)

RealSelf is the dominant purpose-built discovery platform for aesthetic procedures in North America.
Its provider listing structure reveals what NA consumers prioritize:

**Above the fold (immediate trust signals):**
- Overall star rating + total review count (visible instantly)
- "Verified" blue checkmark badge (requires 4+ star rating, board certification, good medical standing)
- Board certification status (listed prominently — RealSelf only surfaces board-certified providers for each procedure)
- Provider specialty and credentials
- Location / distance

**Mid-listing (decision content):**
- "Worth It" rating for specific procedures (binary: Worth It / Not Worth It — procedures need 50+ reviews and 90%+ Worth It rate to rank)
- Before/after photo gallery (visual proof, high weight in decision)
- Treatment-specific reviews (moderated, cannot be removed by doctors)
- Q&A with the provider (demonstrates expertise and responsiveness)

**Lower / supporting:**
- Pricing ranges (where available)
- Training, education, association memberships
- Office photos

**Key structural insight for AgentDock:** NA consumers expect a tiered trust hierarchy — credential badge FIRST, social proof volume SECOND, visual results THIRD, pricing FOURTH. Reversing this order (leading with price) signals discount positioning and erodes confidence.

### 1.2 Zocdoc Patterns (Healthcare/Booking Focus)

Zocdoc (broader healthcare, not aesthetics-only) contributes these structural norms:
- Insurance / payment options listed inline with provider card
- Immediate "Book" button always visible — no friction between decision and action
- Next available appointment slot shown on listing (urgency + convenience signal)
- Patient-verified reviews only (not anonymous)
- Provider photo mandatory — face humanizes the listing

### 1.3 Yelp / Groupon Patterns (Price-Anchored Discovery)

These platforms serve a different segment (price-sensitive, deal-driven):
- Lead with discount percentage and original price
- Star rating + review count dominant
- Category tags ("Med Spa," "Laser Hair Removal," "Injectables")
- Photo gallery prominent
- Map/distance visible at top

**AgentDock distinction:** AgentDock is NOT a deal platform. Its differentiation is structured, comparable listings optimized for AI agent parsing — this aligns with RealSelf/Zocdoc patterns, not Yelp/Groupon. The landing page should explicitly signal this positioning gap.

## SECTION 2 — NA WEB DESIGN CONVENTIONS FOR HEALTHCARE/WELLNESS

### 2.1 Overall Aesthetic Direction

**Dominant paradigm:** Clean-minimal with strategic information density  
NOT information-dense (that signals old healthcare, insurance portals)  
NOT ultra-sparse luxury-only (that signals unaffordability, alienates mainstream)  
SWEET SPOT: Premium-clean with generous whitespace, selective use of clinical detail

**The "luxury clinic" template that dominates in 2025–2026:**
- Full-width hero with high-quality lifestyle/treatment imagery
- Off-white or very light background (#FCFCFC, not pure white)
- Sans-serif typography (Sora, Inter, Helvetica Neue are common)
- Large base font (17px+) with generous line height (32px+)
- 2–3 font maximum
- Sticky navigation with CTA button always visible

### 2.2 Color Palettes

**Trust palette (healthcare authority):**
- Navy/deep blue + white: conveys medical credibility (34% increase in perceived trust per color psychology research)
- Blue + soft gold accent: premium healthcare, common for plastic surgery
- Deep slate + rose: med spa femininity without being "girly"

**Wellness/healing palette:**
- Green (#3A7D44 range) + cream: natural, healing, less clinical
- Sage + white + warm neutrals: spa-adjacent, popular for holistic offerings

**AgentDock relevance:** As a technology-forward platform, a palette of deep navy/slate + clean white + a single accent (gold or teal) would signal both medical credibility and tech sophistication. Avoid aggressive reds or pure-black backgrounds.

### 2.3 Whitespace Usage

Generous whitespace is a TRUST signal in this vertical — it signals premium positioning and reduces anxiety.  
Section padding minimum: 80px top/bottom  
Tight, cluttered layouts are strongly associated with discount/deal platforms (Groupon aesthetic)  
Max content container: 1200–1240px  
Single-column on mobile, intentional two-column on desktop

### 2.4 Hero Section Patterns

**Standard high-converting hero for med aesthetics:**
1. Full-width background: either lifestyle photo (real patient/model, not stock) or clean gradient
2. Headline: benefit-first, outcome-focused (NOT "Welcome to [Clinic Name]")
3. Sub-headline: 1–2 sentences of credibility context
4. Primary CTA button: above fold, high contrast, specific action verb
5. Secondary trust signal: credential badge, patient count, or "board-certified" mention inline

**Hero headline formulas that convert:**
- "Find [City]'s Best [Treatment] Providers — Vetted, Verified, Bookable"
- "Discover Treatments That Work. Clinics You Can Trust."
- Outcome + credibility pairing is the dominant formula

**CTA copy conventions:**
- "Book a Free Consultation" — most common, lowest friction, highest intent
- "Find My Provider" — discovery-focused, works for aggregator platforms
- "Book Now" alone — too transactional for first-touch aesthetics (creates pressure)
- "Start Your Journey" — resonates with empowerment-minded consumers but can feel vague
- RECOMMENDATION for AgentDock: "Explore Verified Clinics" or "Find Clinics Near You" — matches the discovery/comparison positioning without booking pressure

### 2.5 Trust Signals — Specific Elements and Placement

**Tier 1 — Must-Have (absence damages credibility):**
- Board certification logos / badge (ABPS, ABFPRS, etc.)
- HIPAA compliance mention (privacy policy link + footer badge)
- SSL / security indicators (implicit but expected)
- Real staff photos with credentials listed
- Physical address + phone number visible (not just a form)
- Google/Yelp review count + aggregate rating

**Tier 2 — High-Value Differentiators:**
- BBB accreditation logo
- "As Seen In" press mentions (Allure, Vogue, RealSelf)
- Association memberships (AmSpa — American Med Spa Association)
- Patient count ("10,000+ patients served")
- Named medical director with credentials
- Before/after gallery with consent notation

**Tier 3 — Supporting (nice-to-have):**
- Awards and rankings
- Financing partner logos (CareCredit, Alle, Cherry)
- Technology brand logos (Botox, Juvederm, Coolsculpting — brand recognition)

**Placement priority:**
- Above fold: security/SSL implicit, CTA visible
- Hero or just below: 2–3 Tier 1 signals as icon/badge strip
- Mid-page: social proof (review count, before/after)
- Footer: HIPAA badge, privacy policy, physical address, board cert logos

## SECTION 3 — NA CONSUMER BEHAVIOR FOR MED AESTHETICS

### 3.1 The Discovery Journey (7-Stage Model)

Stage 1 — AWARENESS: Social media (TikTok, Instagram) or search trigger  
Stage 2 — CONSIDERATION: Google search, platform browsing, review reading  
Stage 3 — COMPARISON: 63% compare 2–5 providers before deciding  
Stage 4 — DECISION: Final check on credentials, pricing, location convenience  
Stage 5 — BOOKING: Expects online booking; 24/7 availability matters  
Stage 6 — EXPERIENCE: In-clinic  
Stage 7 — ADVOCACY: Reviews, referrals, loyalty

**Critical insight:** The jump from Stage 2 to Stage 3 is where aggregator/discovery platforms (like AgentDock) own the highest-value real estate. Consumers are ACTIVELY comparing — the platform that makes comparison easiest wins the referral.

### 3.2 What Consumers Prioritize (Ranked by Research)

1. **Real user reviews** — 51% cite reviews as most trusted source; 89% check reviews before booking
2. **Provider credentials / board certification** — Required trust signal; absence is disqualifying
3. **Before/after galleries** — Visual proof; "they want to SEE, not be told"
4. **About Us / provider story** — 59% say "About Us" is the most important page on a medspa site
5. **Pricing transparency** — Cost competitiveness is a key Stage 3 decision factor
6. **Location / distance** — 10–15 mile radius dominates patient acquisition
7. **Online booking availability** — Friction in booking = abandonment
8. **Financing options** — CareCredit, Cherry, Alle logos are strong conversion signals for $500+ treatments

### 3.3 Information Accuracy as a Trust Prerequisite

55% of consumers are LESS LIKELY to choose a medspa if they find incorrect info (wrong hours, wrong address) across search profiles and website.  
This is a critical insight for AgentDock: if clinic listings contain stale/inconsistent data, it destroys trust not just in the clinic but in the platform itself.  
The "structured listing" proposition of AgentDock directly addresses this — machine-readable, consistently formatted data is a trust mechanism.

### 3.4 The Comparison Behavior Gap

63% compare 2–5 providers before deciding — but current discovery is fragmented across Google Maps, RealSelf, Yelp, and clinic websites.  
There is NO dominant single platform that enables side-by-side structured comparison (RealSelf comes closest but is review-focused, not comparison-table focused).  
AgentDock's AI-agent comparison layer fills this exact gap — this should be the central value proposition on the landing page.

### 3.5 Emerging Segments (2025–2026)

- **Gen Z "prejuvenation"** cohort: preventive Botox in 20s, highly research-driven, TikTok-native discovery
- **GLP-1 adjacent treatments**: Weight loss patients seeking body contouring, skin tightening post-weight-loss
- **Male consumers**: Growing segment (still minority but fastest-growing)
- **Menopausal wellness**: Hormone-adjacent treatments, vaginal rejuvenation, HRT-adjacent services

## SECTION 4 — COPY / MESSAGING TONE

### 4.1 The Winning Tone Formula

**Framework: Authority-First → Empowerment-Finish**

1. Open with clinical authority (establishes safety and legitimacy)
2. Shift to patient agency language (you decide, you deserve, your confidence)
3. Close with specific outcome promise qualified with appropriate hedging

**What converts:** Educational, reassuring, expert-guided but not paternalistic  
**What repels NA consumers:** Aggressive urgency ("Limited time!"), over-clinical (intimidating), or over-casual (unprofessional)

### 4.2 Specific Language Patterns

**High-converting phrases in this vertical:**
- "Board-certified providers" (credibility anchor)
- "Personalized treatment plans" (individualization signal)
- "Natural-looking results" (2025 dominant outcome preference — "regenerative aesthetics" era)
- "Transparent pricing" (trust + comparison enabler)
- "Free consultation" (lowers commitment threshold)
- "Vetted" / "verified" (platform-level trust transfer)

**Language to avoid:**
- "Guaranteed results" (FTC violation territory)
- "Pain-free" (unqualified claim)
- "Permanent" (unqualified and often inaccurate)
- "Best in [City]" (superlative claims require evidence)
- "Luxury" overuse (can signal price inaccessibility)

### 4.3 E-E-A-T Signals in Copy (Google + Consumer Trust)

Experience: "Over [X] treatments performed"  
Expertise: Named medical director + credentials in-line  
Authoritativeness: Cite certifications, associations, press mentions  
Trustworthiness: Patient testimonials, review counts, HIPAA mention

### 4.4 AgentDock-Specific Messaging Angles

The platform sits at the intersection of two audiences: clinic owners (publishers) and consumers (via AI agents). The landing page needs to serve the clinic owner registration flow primarily.

**For clinic owners (B2B angle):**
- Lead with reach + qualified leads: "Get discovered by AI agents searching for clinics like yours"
- Pain point: "Stop losing patients to platforms that bury your credentials"
- Authority angle: "Structured listings built for the way patients actually search now"
- Specificity: "Your board certifications, before/after gallery, and pricing — one structured profile AI agents can read and compare"

**For consumer-facing copy (B2C/trust transfer):**
- "Every clinic on AgentDock is structured, verified, and AI-comparable"
- "Your AI assistant can compare clinics, credentials, and pricing — in seconds"
- "Discovery designed for how you actually decide"

## SECTION 5 — LEGAL / TRUST REQUIREMENTS

### 5.1 HIPAA Compliance (Required for Any Health-Adjacent Platform)

HIPAA applies whenever a platform handles Protected Health Information (PHI) — which includes:
- Patient names linked to treatments
- Before/after photos
- Booking/appointment data
- Payment records tied to treatments

**Website requirements:**
- Published Privacy Policy (HIPAA-compliant language)
- BAA (Business Associate Agreement) with any third-party tools handling PHI
- Explicit consent before collecting any patient data through forms
- Secure transmission (SSL/TLS) — HTTPS mandatory
- Before/after photo usage REQUIRES written, signed patient consent specifying exact use (website, social media, print)
- HIPAA badge in footer is a VISUAL TRUST SIGNAL even if technically non-certifiable (HIPAA has no official certification body)

**Fines for non-compliance:** Up to $1.5M USD per violation category

### 5.2 FTC Advertising Requirements

The FTC requires all health-related advertising claims to be:
- Truthful and not misleading
- Backed by "competent and reliable scientific evidence"
- Prominently disclosed when material connections exist (paid reviews, influencer posts)

**Specific requirements:**
- "Results may vary" or "Individual results may vary" — required near any before/after imagery or outcome claims
- Disclaimers must be PROMINENT — not buried in footer fine print, not hidden in hashtags
- Testimonials must reflect typical results OR clearly disclose they are atypical
- Paid partnerships / sponsored content must be disclosed with "#ad" or "Paid Partnership" — not optional

### 5.3 FDA Advertising Standards

- Promoted devices/injectables must be FDA-cleared or approved for the specific use advertised
- Off-label promotion of FDA-approved drugs is prohibited in advertising
- Claims like "permanent results," "pain-free," "clinically proven" require substantiation
- Technology brand names (Botox, Juvederm, Coolsculpting) carry their own advertising restrictions from the manufacturer

### 5.4 State-Level Medical Advertising Rules

- Medical boards in most US states regulate physician advertising independently
- Many states prohibit "best," "top," or "number one" superlatives without substantiation
- Some states require disclosure of provider type (MD vs. NP vs. PA performing treatments)
- Canada (for CA coverage): Provincial colleges regulate advertising similarly; implied endorsement rules apply

### 5.5 Required Page Elements (Compliance Checklist)

**Must-have on any US med aesthetics platform:**
- [ ] Privacy Policy page (HIPAA-compliant language)
- [ ] Terms of Service
- [ ] "Results may vary / Individual results may vary" near all before/after content
- [ ] Disclaimers near outcome claims (not buried)
- [ ] Physical address + contact information (FTC requires for health businesses)
- [ ] Cookie consent banner (CCPA for California users)
- [ ] Disclosure if any reviews are incentivized
- [ ] Patient photo consent statement near gallery content
- [ ] SSL certificate (HTTPS)
- [ ] Medical director identification with credentials

**Recommended (trust signal + partial compliance buffer):**
- [ ] HIPAA compliance badge / statement in footer
- [ ] BBB accreditation logo
- [ ] AmSpa membership badge (American Med Spa Association)
- [ ] "Not a substitute for professional medical advice" near educational content
- [ ] Financing partner logos (CareCredit etc.) with "subject to credit approval" notation

- HIPAA compliance statement
- Privacy Policy + Terms of Service
- Physical address + contact

### 6.2 Copy Tone Guidance Summary

- Voice: Expert-neutral, tech-forward, empowering (not salesy, not overly clinical)
- Reading level: 8th–10th grade (Flesch-Kincaid target 60–70)
- Sentence length: Short (15 words avg). No paragraph blocks over 3 sentences.
- Headers: Outcome-focused, not feature-focused
- Avoid: Jargon ("omnichannel"), superlatives without backing, pressure urgency

### 6.3 Key Differentiators to Foreground

1. Structured data = AI-readable (no other platform offers this explicitly)
2. Credential-first hierarchy (matches what 89% of consumers actually verify)
3. Comparison-native (addresses the 63%-compare-before-deciding behavior gap)
4. Compliance scaffolding built in (consent workflow, HIPAA-aware data handling)
5. Both audiences served: clinic owners get qualified leads; consumers get AI-mediated comparison
