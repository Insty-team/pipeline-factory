# Track B — Market Research and Pain-Point Exploration

Date: 2026-04-03
Question:
> What pain point can generate revenue quickly while also having structural upside?

## Evaluation criteria
Every new hypothesis should score well on as many of these as possible:
1. The problem is obvious.
2. There is a buyer who can pay now.
3. Results show up quickly.
4. The work is repeatable / scalable.
5. The pitch is easy to understand.

## External signals used in this memo
### AI-native shopping / product discovery is becoming real
- OpenAI says more people are starting shopping in ChatGPT and compares products there.
- OpenAI says merchants can share product feeds/promotions so catalogs are “fully represented in ChatGPT.”
- OpenAI merchant page says feeds improve catalog coverage, richer details, accuracy, and freshness; it also explicitly says feeds give greater control than crawling alone.

### Local discovery still matters a lot
- Google’s LocalBusiness docs still emphasize structured local business data, crawlability, recrawling, and sitemap submission.
- Google’s local search behavior research found 4 in 5 consumers use search engines for local information and local searches convert faster than non-local searches.

### Small businesses want AI help, but most are not deeply integrated
- Goldman Sachs’ March 17, 2026 survey says 76% of small businesses report using AI, but only 14% have fully integrated it into core operations; 73% want more training and implementation help.

### Beauty remains large and growing
- McKinsey’s 2025 beauty report projects beauty core segments to reach $590B by 2030 and notes growth continues shifting online.

### Returns are a huge operational pain in e-commerce
- NRF said retailers expect 15.8% of annual sales to be returned in 2025, totaling $849.9B.

## Candidate Hypotheses

## Hypothesis 1 — Pre-purchase qualification / wrong-choice reduction for e-commerce
### One-line version
Help merchants reduce wrong purchases, repetitive pre-purchase questions, and returns by turning messy product information into structured, decision-ready buying guidance.

### Why this is attractive
This pain is tied directly to money:
- fewer returns
- fewer support tickets
- more confident conversions

And unlike a pure SEO story, it can show value quickly in:
- support volume
- conversion rate
- return rate
- product-page engagement

### Why now
- OpenAI is explicitly building product discovery and feed-driven shopping.
- ChatGPT merchant docs say richer feeds improve coverage, detail, accuracy, and freshness.
- NRF says returns remain extremely costly at scale.

### What the product could look like
Input:
- product pages
- PDP copy
- FAQs
- return reasons
- reviews / common pre-purchase questions

Output:
- canonical product decision record
- buyer-facing fit / use-case guidance
- AI-readable product data layer
- pre-purchase Q&A blocks
- structured data / feed cleanup
- tracked reduction in wrong-choice traffic / support / returns

### Score
- problem clarity: 5
- buyer immediacy: 4
- time to value: 4
- repeatability: 5
- ease of explanation: 4

**Total: 22/25**

### Why this could become structurally large
Because the same system can expand from service to:
- feed ops
- product knowledge graph
- reusable comparison content
- AI shopping optimization layer

### Main risk
Crowded space; many players claim “AI ecommerce optimization.”
The wedge must be very specific, for example:
- high-return categories
- high-inquiry categories
- complex beauty / wellness products
- wrong-product-selection reduction

### 7-day falsification test
- interview 10 merchants in a high-return or high-inquiry category
- ask for common pre-purchase questions + return reasons
- see whether 3+ will share URLs / question logs / return reasons
- if yes, this becomes a serious Track B candidate

## Hypothesis 2 — Merchant feed / catalog cleanup for AI shopping discovery
### One-line version
Help merchants make their catalog more accurate and more fully represented in AI shopping/search systems by fixing feed quality, product structure, and freshness.

### Why this is attractive
OpenAI’s merchant docs explicitly say:
- product feeds improve how products appear in ChatGPT
- feeds expand catalog coverage
- feeds share richer details
- richer data helps products surface with more accurate information
- feeds keep pricing and availability updated

This is a very direct signal that a real infrastructure layer is emerging.

### What the product could look like
Input:
- merchant catalog / product feed / store data

Output:
- normalized product feed
- schema + feed cleanup
- missing attributes fixed
- category / variant cleanup
- quality checks for AI shopping surfaces
- submission / monitoring workflow

### Score
- problem clarity: 4
- buyer immediacy: 4
- time to value: 3
- repeatability: 5
- ease of explanation: 4

**Total: 20/25**

### Why this could become structurally large
If AI shopping expands, this becomes analogous to:
- SEO technical hygiene
- merchant center operations
- feed ops for AI channels

This could compound because once you solve the feed model for one merchant type, the service becomes much more repeatable.

### Main risk
- platform dependence
- some merchants (for example many Shopify merchants) may already be partially covered
- easier for technical teams to internalize over time

### 7-day falsification test
- talk to 8–10 small/medium brands selling across multiple channels
- ask whether they know how their catalog appears in ChatGPT shopping or other AI shopping surfaces
- ask if they already have clean feeds / structured data ownership
- if most have no owner and no confidence, this becomes stronger

## Hypothesis 3 — High-ticket local service canonical discovery layer
### One-line version
Help local high-ticket service businesses (clinics, dental, aesthetics, legal, home services) structure their official branch/service information so AI/search comparisons are less distorted and booking-ready.

### Why this is attractive
- local intent is high-value
- branch / service / pricing / booking details are often messy
- Google still rewards structured local information and booking connectivity
- local searches are historically action-heavy

### What the product could look like
Input:
- official branch pages
- service pages
- booking pages
- contact/location pages

Output:
- branch-level canonical records
- service-level pages
- booking-ready public pages
- local structured data
- before/after discoverability tracking

### Score
- problem clarity: 4
- buyer immediacy: 3
- time to value: 3
- repeatability: 4
- ease of explanation: 3

**Total: 17/25**

### Why this might still matter
This is likely the current clinic thesis. It may monetize, but it is slower, more consultative, and more sales-heavy than the top two candidates.

### Main risk
- less explosive
- more trust-heavy
- longer proof cycle
- easier to collapse into “SEO/website cleanup” in the buyer’s mind

### 7-day falsification test
- the current Muse / Shinebeam outreach is already a live test
- strongest pass signal = URL sharing or draft review willingness

## Hypothesis 4 — Broad SMB AI implementation concierge
### One-line version
Offer AI implementation help to small businesses that want efficiency gains but lack the expertise to deploy AI well.

### Why it appears attractive
Goldman’s survey shows:
- 76% use AI
- only 14% are fully integrated
- 73% want more training and implementation help

### Why I would **not** prioritize it now
This is too broad.
It fails the “easy explanation + repeatability” test unless narrowly verticalized.
It also tends to become classic consulting very quickly.

### Score
- problem clarity: 3
- buyer immediacy: 3
- time to value: 2
- repeatability: 2
- ease of explanation: 2

**Total: 12/25**

### Recommendation
Reject for now unless narrowed sharply into one workflow, one role, one vertical.

## Interpretation
### The strongest Track B opportunities look more like:
- commerce
- decision support
- feed / catalog infrastructure
- conversion or returns economics

than like:
- generic AI consulting
- broad local-discovery services
- design-heavy deliverables

This suggests a useful tension:
- **Track A** may be better for immediate narrow testing
- **Track B** may contain higher-upside models if we can find a wedge with faster feedback loops and clearer economics

---

## Best next exploration questions
If we continue Track B, the highest-value questions are:

### For hypothesis 1
- Which categories have the worst “wrong choice” economics?
- Which buyers already feel enough pain to pay quickly?
- What can be improved in 7 days that a merchant immediately understands?

### For hypothesis 2
- Which merchant segments are not already well-covered by Shopify / existing feed tooling?
- Where is feed ownership unclear or neglected?
- Can we position around “control and accuracy in AI shopping” rather than generic feed operations?

### For hypothesis 3
- Is there a narrower local-service wedge with stronger urgency than clinics?
- Could booking or lead qualification make the ROI story more immediate?

---

## Recommendation
If Track B is explored further, start with these two:
1. **wrong-choice / pre-purchase qualification in commerce**
2. **merchant feed / catalog cleanup for AI shopping discovery**

Keep Track A running only as a bounded one-month test.
Do not let broad Track B exploration disrupt that execution.
